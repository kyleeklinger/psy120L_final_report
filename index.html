Introduction & Overview

Context

The report documents the workflow from the original Qualtrics survey data to study variables, scale scoring, descriptive statistics, reliability checks, and final statistical analyses.

The purpose of this report is to make the analysis process clear so that a reader (of the manuscript-style research paper) can understand how the data were prepared and how the final results were produced.

Study Overview

The current study examined the effects of feedback type (autonomy-supportive or controlling) on students’ self-efficacy and achievement motivation, and how parental warmth influences these relationships.

Design:

  • 1 manipulated independent variable (autonomy-supportive or controlling feedback)

  • 1 moderating variable (high or low perceived parental warmth)

  • 2 dependent variables (self-efficacy and achievement motivation)

The primary analyses in this report used two 2 × 2 ANOVAs to test the main and interaction effects of feedback type and parental warmth on self-efficacy and achievement motivation.

Assigning Level of Perceived Parental Warmth

Assigning Parenting Style

Using the highest score of the three PAQ subscales to assign parenting style; authoritative, authoritarian, or permissive. A tie is assigned when scores are the same for two different styles.

Code 
lab_data <- lab_data %>%
  mutate(parenting_style = case_when(
    ATNPAQ_scale > ATEPAQ_scale & ATNPAQ_scale > PerPAQ_scale ~ "Authoritarian",
    ATEPAQ_scale > ATNPAQ_scale & ATEPAQ_scale > PerPAQ_scale ~ "Authoritative",
    PerPAQ_scale > ATNPAQ_scale & PerPAQ_scale > ATEPAQ_scale ~ "Permissive",
    TRUE ~ "Tie"
  ))

Results Summary

The primary analyses tested whether feedback condition, parental warmth, and their interaction predicted self-efficacy and achievement motivation scores. For both 2 × 2 ANOVAs run, feedback condition was not a statistically significant predictor of either dependent variable.

These results indicate that participants who received autonomy-supportive feedback did not significantly differ in self-efficacy or achievement motivation compared to participants who received controlling feedback. Participants with high or low parental warmth also did not significantly differ in levels of self-efficacy and achievement motivation.

The main hypotheses were not statistically supported because feedback type and level of parental warmth did not independently predict self-efficacy and achievement motivation. Similarly, the interaction hypothesis was not supported because parental warmth did not significantly moderate the effects of the feedback type.
